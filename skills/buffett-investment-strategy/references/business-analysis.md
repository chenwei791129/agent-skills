# Business Analysis Framework

## The Moat Taxonomy

### 1. Brand / Consumer Franchise
A true franchise exists when: (1) the product is needed or desired, (2) customers perceive no close substitute, and (3) it is not subject to price regulation. (1991)

- **Examples**: Coca-Cola, See's Candies, Gillette
- **Test**: "Is it really so difficult to conclude that Coca-Cola and Gillette possess far less business risk over the long term than, say, any computer company or retailer?" (1993)
- **Key insight**: "The might of their brand names, the attributes of their products, and the strength of their distribution systems give them an enormous competitive advantage, setting up a protective moat around their economic castles." (1993)

### 2. Low-Cost Producer
Sustainable cost advantage that competitors cannot replicate easily.

- **Examples**: GEICO (direct insurance model), Nebraska Furniture Mart, Costco
- **Test**: "One question I always ask myself in appraising a business is how I would like, assuming I had ample capital and skilled personnel, to compete with it." (1983)
- **Key insight**: GEICO and Costco share a "holy duty to have a wonderful product at a very low price. A lot of people talk that game, but very few have it just right down under the body and soul of the company." (Munger, 2014)

### 3. Network Effects / Switching Costs
Businesses where customer value increases with scale or where leaving is costly.

- **Examples**: American Express (merchant network), Apple (ecosystem lock-in)
- **Test**: Would customers switch for a 10% price cut? If not, the moat is real.

### 4. Regulatory / Natural Monopoly
Businesses protected by regulation or geography.

- **Examples**: BNSF Railway, BHE utilities
- **Key insight**: "You can't expect to earn outsized profits in regulated utilities, but the industry offers owners the opportunity to deploy large sums at fair returns." (2005)

## Franchise vs Commodity Business

| Dimension | Franchise | Commodity |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Pricing power | Can raise prices above inflation | Price-taker |
| Capital needs | Low tangible assets | Capital intensive |
| Returns on equity | High and stable | Cyclical, often inadequate |
| Competitive dynamics | Moat protects margins | Overcapacity destroys returns |
| Management impact | Good is amplified | Good barely maintains |

> "This works with candy bars (customers buy by brand name, not by asking for a 'two-ounce candy bar') but doesn't work with sugar." (1982)

> "The textile industry illustrates in textbook style how producers of relatively undifferentiated goods in capital intensive businesses must earn inadequate returns except under conditions of tight supply or real shortage." (1978)

## The Capital Intensity Trap

In commodity industries, each company's capex appears rational individually, but collectively lowers the cost baseline for everyone without improving returns. Burlington Industries invested $3B over 21 years with nearly flat stock price. (1985)

> "A good managerial record (measured by economic returns) is far more a function of what business boat you get into than it is of how effectively you row." (1985)

## Three Types of Businesses (2007)

1. **Great**: Earns high returns on tangible capital and requires little reinvestment. Like a savings account paying extraordinary and rising interest.
2. **Good**: Earns high returns but requires significant reinvestment to grow. Returns on incremental capital are still attractive.
3. **Terrible**: Requires significant capital but earns low returns. Like a savings account paying inadequate interest that also requires increasing deposits.

> "The best business to own is one that over an extended period can employ large amounts of incremental capital at very high rates of return. The worst business to own is one that must, or will, do the opposite." (1992)

> "The best business is one that gives you more and more money every year without putting up anything to get it, or very little." (Buffett, 1998 Annual Meeting)

## Tailwinds vs Headwinds

> "One of the lessons your management has learned — and, unfortunately, sometimes re-learned — is the importance of being in businesses where tailwinds prevail rather than headwinds." (1977)

In a tailwind industry, even average management produces good results.
In a headwind industry, even brilliant management barely survives.

## The Moat Widening Test

Every day, every business's competitive position is either strengthening or weakening.

> "Every day, in countless ways, the competitive position of each of our businesses grows either weaker or stronger... widening the moat must take precedence." (2005)

> "A truly great business must have an enduring 'moat' that protects excellent returns on invested capital... A moat that must be continuously rebuilt will eventually be no moat at all." (2007)

## Lost Moats Cannot Be Recovered

Once a brand or competitive position is compromised, recovery is nearly impossible.

> Packard automobiles descended from premium to discount, and "never regained that upscale image again." (Buffett, 2003 Annual Meeting)

## Economic Goodwill vs Accounting Goodwill

Businesses with high returns on low tangible assets possess economic goodwill far exceeding book value. See's Candies earned 25% on $8M tangible assets — the economic goodwill was the real asset. In inflationary environments, economic goodwill is "the gift that keeps giving." (1983)

## Insurance as a Business Model

Insurance is fundamentally a commodity (standardized policies anyone can copy), but individual management quality is amplified enormously because "there is no question that the nature of the insurance business magnifies the effect which individual managers have on company performance." (1977)

### The Four Disciplines of Insurance Underwriting

1. Accept only risks that can be properly evaluated
2. Limit aggregate exposure from any single event
3. Avoid moral hazard
4. Do not follow competitors into underpriced markets — "Many insurers pass the first three tests and flunk the fourth." (2011)

### Float Economics

Float = premiums collected before claims are paid. If underwriting is profitable, float is better than free money — you get paid to hold it.

- 1967: $19 million float
- 1999: $25.3 billion
- 2012: $73.1 billion
- 2024: $171 billion

> "It's like running a bank where people leave their money with you and you pay a minus 2.2% and you don't have any check clearing or anything else to do." (Buffett, 2025 Annual Meeting)
