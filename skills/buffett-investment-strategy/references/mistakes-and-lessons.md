# Mistakes and Lessons

## Categories of Mistakes

### Type 1: Sins of Commission (Doing Something Wrong)

#### The Cigar Butt Trap
Buying statistically cheap, fundamentally bad businesses. The "one free puff" rarely compensates for the trouble of holding a deteriorating business.

> "Both our operating and investment experience cause us to conclude that 'turnarounds' seldom turn, and that the same energies and talent are much better employed in a good business purchased at a fair price than in a poor business purchased at a bargain price." (1979)

**Case: Berkshire Hathaway Textile Operations (1965-1985)**
The original sin. Buffett bought a declining textile business because it was cheap. Spent 20 years trying to make it work before finally closing it. Looms bought for $25,000 sold as scrap for $26 each.

> "When a management with a reputation for brilliance tackles a business with a reputation for poor fundamental economics, it is the reputation of the business that remains intact." (1980)

#### Paying with Undervalued Currency
**Case: Dexter Shoe (1993)**
Paid $433M in Berkshire stock for a shoe company that later proved worthless (zero moat against foreign competition). Those shares would have been worth $5.7B.

> "The most gruesome was Dexter Shoe... the shares I used for the purchase are now worth about $5.7 billion. As a financial disaster, this one deserves a spot in the Guinness Book of World Records." (2014)

**Case: General Re (1998)**
Used Berkshire stock (diluting shareholders by 21.8%) to acquire General Re, which brought hidden derivative liabilities and underwriting problems.

#### Sloppy Analysis on "Safe" Securities
**Case: USAir Preferred Stock (1989)**
Invested $358M in preferred stock of a troubled airline. The airline industry's terrible economics should have been obvious.

> "This was a case of sloppy analysis, a lapse that may have been caused by the fact that we were buying a senior security or by hubris." (1994)

#### Thumb-Sucking (Delayed Action on Known Mistakes)
**Case: Tesco (2012-2014)**
Bought a large position in a UK retailer showing deteriorating fundamentals. Delayed selling while problems worsened, losing $444M.

> "The cardinal sin is delaying the correction of mistakes or what Charlie Munger called 'thumb-sucking.'" (2024)

> "You don't have to make it back the way that you lost it." (1994)

#### Overpaying for a Good Business
**Case: Precision Castparts (2016)**
Paid $37B for an excellent aerospace parts maker — at the top of the cycle. Wrote down $11B in 2020.

> "I paid too much for the company." (2020)

#### Reckless Expansion Without Knowledge
**Case: Home & Auto Insurance — Florida Expansion (1974)**
Entered a new market without proper underwriting data or pricing knowledge. Lost over $2M.

> "In retrospect, it is apparent that our management simply did not have the underwriting information and the pricing knowledge necessary to be operating in the area." (1974)

### Type 2: Sins of Omission (Failing to Act)

These are consistently described as the MORE costly category of mistakes.

> "Typically, our most egregious mistakes fall in the omission, rather than the commission, category." (1991)

**Case: Walmart**
Stopped buying because the price rose a small amount from initial purchase. Estimated opportunity cost: $8B+.

**Case: Fannie Mae**
Understood the opportunity, started buying, didn't finish. Lost approximately $1.4B in potential gains.

**Case: Amazon / Google / etc.**
Understood the business quality but couldn't get comfortable with valuation within circle of competence.

> "Pharmaceuticals, we missed... we certainly should've recognized — did recognize, didn't do anything about it." / "Yeah, we stupidly blew that one." (Buffett & Munger, 1998 Annual Meeting)

### Type 3: Institutional Imperative Errors

When otherwise rational managers follow the crowd.

> "I thought then that decent, intelligent, and experienced managers would automatically make rational business decisions. But I learned over time that isn't so." (1989)

> "The tendency of executives to mindlessly imitate the behavior of their peers, no matter how foolish it may be to do so." (1990)

> "The acquisition problem is often compounded by a biological bias: Many CEO's attain their positions in part because they possess an abundance of animal spirits and ego." (1994)

**The Toad-Kissing Delusion:**
> "Investors can always buy toads at the going price for toads. If investors instead bankroll princesses who wish to pay double for the right to kiss the toad, those kisses had better pack some real dynamite. We've observed many kisses but very few miracles." (1981)

## Key Lessons Distilled

### 1. Business Quality Trumps Price
Time is the friend of wonderful businesses and the enemy of mediocre ones. A fair price for excellence beats a bargain price for mediocrity every time.

### 2. Act Decisively on Clear Opportunities
The cost of inaction on obvious opportunities far exceeds the cost of occasional mistakes. When you see a fat pitch, swing hard.

### 3. Admit Mistakes Quickly and Move On
> "During the 2019-23 period, I have used the words 'mistake' or 'error' 16 times in my letters to you. Many other huge companies have never used either word." (2024)

### 4. Never Pay with Undervalued Stock
If you must acquire, use cash. Paying with undervalued shares turns a mediocre deal into a catastrophic one.

### 5. The Business Boat Matters More Than Rowing Skill
> "A good managerial record (measured by economic returns) is far more a function of what business boat you get into than it is of how effectively you row." (1985)

### 6. Beware the Advisory-Industrial Complex
> "That's like asking your interior decorator whether you need a $50,000 rug." (1994, on consulting investment bankers about acquisitions)

### 7. Anchoring Kills Returns
Don't let your purchase price dictate future decisions. If the analysis says buy more, buy more — regardless of what you already paid.

### 8. Avoid the Half-Awake Problem
> "You do not adequately protect yourself by being half awake while others are sleeping. It was a mistake to buy fifteen-year bonds, and yet we did; we made an even more serious mistake in not selling them (at losses, if necessary) when our present views began to crystallize." (1979)

### 9. Focus on One-Foot Hurdles
> "We concentrated on identifying one-foot hurdles that we could step over rather than because we acquired any ability to clear seven-footers." (1989)

### 10. The Real Risk in Due Diligence
> "The mistakes are always about making an improper assessment of the economic conditions in the future of the industry of the company. They're not a bad lease... Those are not the things that count." (Buffett, 2016 Annual Meeting)
