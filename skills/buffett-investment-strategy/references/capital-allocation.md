# Capital Allocation Principles

## The Hierarchy of Capital Deployment

Buffett's priority order for deploying cash:

1. **Reinvest in existing businesses** — if returns on incremental capital are attractive
2. **Acquire new businesses** — meeting the six acquisition criteria
3. **Buy marketable securities** — partial ownership of outstanding businesses at attractive prices
4. **Repurchase shares** — when price is significantly below intrinsic value
5. **Pay dividends** — only if the above options cannot deploy capital at above-average returns

> "Over time, the skill with which a company's managers allocate capital has an enormous impact on the enterprise's value." (1994)

## Acquisition Criteria (The Six Filters)

1. **Large purchases** (at least $75M pre-tax earnings, preferably much more)
2. **Demonstrated consistent earning power** (future projections and turnarounds are of no interest)
3. **Businesses earning good returns on equity while employing little or no debt**
4. **Management in place** (Berkshire cannot supply it)
5. **Simple businesses** (if there's lots of technology, it won't be understood)
6. **An offering price** (don't waste time on discussions without a price)

> "We will not engage in unfriendly takeovers. We can promise complete confidentiality and a very fast answer — customarily within five minutes." (1982)

> "It's far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price." (1989)

### What Makes Berkshire Different as an Acquirer

- Permanent home: "As an important matter of preferred conduct, Berkshire would almost never sell a subsidiary." (2014, Munger)
- Autonomy preserved: "Just run your business as if: 1) you own 100% of it; 2) it is the only asset in the world that you and your family have or will ever have; and 3) you can't sell or merge it for at least a century." (1998)
- Culture retention: "We do not tell .400 hitters how to swing." (1993)
- No layoffs from volume fluctuations: "We would rather have some slack in the organization from time to time than keep everyone terribly busy writing business on which we are going to lose money." (1979)

## Stock Buyback Principles

### When Buybacks Are Smart
> "When companies with outstanding businesses and comfortable financial positions find their shares selling far below intrinsic value in the marketplace, no alternative action can benefit shareholders as surely as repurchases." (1984)

### The Two Conditions
> "There is only one combination of facts that makes it advisable for a company to repurchase its shares: First, the company has available funds beyond the near-term needs of the business and, second, finds its stock selling in the market below its intrinsic value, conservatively-calculated." (1999)

### The Cardinal Rule
> "The first law of capital allocation — whether the money is slated for acquisitions or share repurchases — is that what is smart at one price is dumb at another." (2011)

> "My suggestion: Before even discussing repurchases, a CEO and his or her Board should stand, join hands and in unison declare, 'What is smart at one price is stupid at another.'" (2016)

### Why Long-Term Holders Should Welcome Buybacks at Low Prices
> "The logic is simple: If you are going to be a net buyer of stocks in the future... you are hurt when stocks rise. You benefit when stocks swoon." (2011)

### Misguided Buybacks
> "Buying dollar bills for $1.10 is not good business for those who stick around." (1999)
> "When you are told that all repurchases are harmful to shareholders or to the country... you are listening to either an economic illiterate or a silver-tongued demagogue." (2022)

## Dividend Policy

Retain earnings when they can be redeployed at above-average returns. Distribute when they cannot.

> "Unrestricted earnings should be retained only when there is a reasonable prospect — backed preferably by historical evidence — that for every dollar retained by the corporation, at least one dollar of market value will be created for owners." (1984)

> "Berkshire would not pay dividends so long as more than one dollar of market value for shareholders was being created by each dollar of retained earnings." (2014, Munger)

## Retained Earnings as Compounding Engine

> "Retained earnings have propelled American business throughout our country's history. What worked for Carnegie and Rockefeller has, over the years, worked its magic for millions of shareholders as well." (2020)

> "For sixty years, Berkshire shareholders endorsed continuous reinvestment and that enabled the company to build its taxable income." (2024)

## Concentration vs Diversification

> "Conventional diversification makes no sense for you... I cannot understand why an investor of that sort elects to put money into a business that is his 20th favorite rather than simply adding that money to his top choices." (1993)

> "We try to avoid buying a little of this or that when we are only lukewarm about the business or its price. When we are convinced as to attractiveness, we believe in buying worthwhile amounts." (1978)

> "In the words of the prophet Mae West: 'Too much of a good thing can be wonderful.'" (1993)

However, for most individual investors who cannot analyze businesses:
> Buffett repeatedly recommended low-cost S&P 500 index funds and proved via a million-dollar bet that hedge funds underperform passive indexing.

## Partial Ownership Can Beat Full Ownership

> "Our experience has been that pro-rata portions of truly outstanding businesses sometimes sell in the securities markets at very large discounts from the prices they would command in negotiated transactions involving entire companies." (1977)

> "In effect, we can obtain a better management result through non-control than control. This is an unorthodox view, but one we believe to be sound." (1977)

## Never Pay with Undervalued Stock

> "We will not issue shares unless we receive as much intrinsic business value as we give." (1982)

The Dexter Shoe lesson: paid $433M in Berkshire stock for a business worth zero. Those shares later worth $5.7B. "As a financial disaster, this one deserves a spot in the Guinness Book of World Records." (2014)

## Cash Management

> "Cash is to a business as oxygen is to an individual: never thought about when it is present, the only thing in mind when it is absent." (2014)

> "Charlie and I never will operate Berkshire in a manner that depends on the kindness of strangers." (2017)

> "We will never prefer ownership of cash-equivalent assets over the ownership of good businesses." (2024)

Berkshire maintains $30B+ minimum cash reserves, primarily in short-term Treasury bills.

## Tax-Efficient Investing

> "Tax-paying investors will realize a far, far greater sum from a single investment that compounds internally at a given rate than from a succession of investments compounding at the same rate." (1993)

> "Charlie and I would follow a buy-and-hold policy even if we ran a tax-exempt institution. We think it the soundest way to invest." (1993)

Example: At 35% tax rate, $1 compounded 20 times with annual sale/repurchase = $22,370. Same $1 compounded 20 times and sold once = $692,000. (1989)

## The Conglomerate Advantage (When Done Right)

> "If the conglomerate form is used judiciously, it is an ideal structure for maximizing long-term capital growth." (2014)

> "At Berkshire, we can — without incurring taxes or much in the way of other costs — move huge sums from businesses that have limited opportunities for incremental investment to other sectors with greater promise." (2014)

Most conglomerates fail because of empire-building CEOs and value-destroying acquisitions. Berkshire succeeds through rational capital allocation and extreme decentralization.

## Opportunity Doesn't Knock on Schedule

> "Charlie always pointed out that we made most of our money out of about eight or nine ideas over 50 years." (Buffett, 2025 Annual Meeting)

> "Things get extraordinarily attractive very occasionally. The long-term trend is up. But nobody knows what the market is going to do tomorrow, next week, or next month. Nobody knows. But they spend all their time talking about it because it's easy to talk about, though it has no value." (Buffett, 2025 Annual Meeting)

> "In the fields of investments and acquisitions, frenetic behavior is often counterproductive." (1998)
