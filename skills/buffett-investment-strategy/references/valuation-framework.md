# Valuation Framework

## Core Definition: Intrinsic Value

> "We define intrinsic value as the discounted value of the cash that can be taken out of a business during its remaining life." (1994)

> "The value of any stock, bond or business today is determined by the cash inflows and outflows — discounted at an appropriate interest rate — that can be expected to occur during the remaining life of the asset." (1992, citing John Burr Williams)

> "Intrinsic value is the present value of the stream of cash that's going to be generated by any financial asset between now and doomsday. And that's easy to say and impossible to figure." (Buffett, 1998 Annual Meeting)

## Owner Earnings (Not GAAP, Not EBITDA)

Owner Earnings = Reported Earnings + Depreciation/Amortization + Other Non-Cash Charges - Average Annual Maintenance Capex

This is what an owner can actually extract from the business while maintaining its competitive position.

> "The one figure we regard as utter nonsense is the so-called EBITDA." (Buffett, 1998 Annual Meeting)

> "EBITDA, a flawed favorite of Wall Street, is not for us." (2024)

## ROE Over EPS

The primary performance metric is return on equity capital employed (without excessive leverage), NOT earnings per share growth.

> "The primary test of managerial economic performance is the achievement of a high earnings rate on equity capital employed (without undue leverage, accounting gimmickry, etc.) and not the achievement of consistent gains in earnings per share." (1979)

> "Most companies define 'record' earnings as a new high in earnings per share. Since businesses customarily add from year to year to their equity base, we find nothing particularly noteworthy in a management performance combining, say, a 10% increase in equity capital and a 5% increase in earnings per share. After all, even a totally dormant savings account will produce steadily rising interest earnings each year because of compounding." (1977)

## The Private Owner Test

> "Our equity investments are heavily concentrated in a few companies which are selected based on favorable economic characteristics, competent and honest management, and a purchase price attractive when measured against the yardstick of value to a private owner." (1975)

Ask: "If I were buying the entire business, what would I pay?" Market prices for fractional interests often diverge wildly from full-business valuations.

> "It seems quite clear to us that either corporations are making very significant mistakes in purchasing entire businesses at prices prevailing in negotiated transactions and takeover bids, or that we eventually are going to make considerable sums of money buying small portions of such businesses at the greatly discounted valuations prevailing in the stock market." (1978)

## Margin of Safety

> "We insist on a margin of safety in our purchase price... We believe this margin-of-safety principle, so strongly emphasized by Ben Graham, to be the cornerstone of investment success." (1992)

The more uncertain the future cash flows, the larger the margin of safety required. Certainty determines the appropriate discount rate:

> "The more certain we feel about a business, the closer we are willing to play it." (Buffett, 1994 Annual Meeting)

## Balance Sheet First

> "I spend more time looking at balance sheets than I do income statements. Wall Street doesn't pay much attention to balance sheets, but I like to look at balance sheets over an 8 or 10 year period before I even look at the income account because there are certain things it's harder to hide or play games with on the balance sheet." (Buffett, 2025 Annual Meeting)

## Look-Through Earnings

GAAP only records dividends received from <20% holdings. Buffett counts the full proportional share of investee earnings to capture economic reality.

Look-Through Earnings = Reported Operating Earnings + Berkshire's Share of Investees' Retained Earnings - Tax on Those Earnings if Distributed

> "The goal of each investor should be to create a portfolio (in effect, a 'company') that will deliver him or her the highest possible look-through earnings a decade or so from now." (1991)

## Book Value vs Intrinsic Value

> "Book value is an accounting concept, recording the accumulated financial input from both contributed capital and retained earnings. Intrinsic business value is an economic concept, estimating future cash output discounted to present value." (1983)

Book value is a useful but imperfect tracking metric. For businesses with significant economic goodwill (See's, Coca-Cola), intrinsic value far exceeds book value. For asset-heavy businesses with poor returns, intrinsic value may be below book value.

## The Dollar Test for Retained Earnings

> "We test the wisdom of retaining earnings by assessing whether retention, over time, delivers shareholders at least $1 of market value for each $1 retained." (1983)

If the company cannot pass this test over a reasonable period, earnings should be distributed via dividends or buybacks.

## GAAP Is the Beginning, Not the End

> "Managers and investors alike must understand that accounting numbers are the beginning, not the end, of business valuation." (1982)

Three key questions financial reports should help answer:
1. Approximately how much is this company worth?
2. Can it meet its future obligations?
3. How well are managers performing?

## Beware of Accounting Manipulation

> "Son of Gresham: Bad accounting drives out good." (1998)

> "Managing may be giving way to manipulating; integrity may be losing out to illusion." (1998, quoting Arthur Levitt)

> "When the accounting confuses you, I would just tend to forget about it as a company... We have never had any great investment results from companies whose accounting we regarded as suspect." (Buffett, 1995 Annual Meeting)

## The Aesop Framework

> "The oracle was Aesop and his enduring, though somewhat incomplete, investment insight was 'a bird in the hand is worth two in the bush.'" (2000)

Three questions complete the framework:
1. How certain are you the birds are in the bush?
2. When will they emerge, and how many?
3. What is the risk-free interest rate (the opportunity cost)?
